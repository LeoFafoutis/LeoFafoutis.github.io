Leo Fafoutis MSML602¶

Introduction¶

Valorant is a 5v5 tactical first-person shooter from Riot Games. It involves a variety of different ‘agents’ (characters to choose from) and has two phases, attack and defend. In both phases, players will try to either eliminate the enemy team, or plant/defuse the ‘spike’ to win a round. Each agent has a unique set of abilities with only 1 player per team being able to select each.

In most competitive esports, there is a term called ‘meta’. This defines the best selection of agents to play during a certain era of the game based on how strong the agent's abilities are. However, there are many cases of teams winning with ‘off-meta’ team compositions or perceived metas being different across certain regions of play.

In this tutorial, we will try to gain a better understanding of the data science pipeline and try to answer the question, ‘what is meta, and does it exist?’. Dataset

    if isinstance(round_history, str):
        round_history = eval(round_history)
    if isinstance(round_history, dict):
        last_round = max(round_history.keys())
        return round_history[last_round]['RoundWinner']
    return None

Now that we have our dataset ready, we can start by building and testing a model. Although we have a fair bit of data, we have a relatively low feature count. Choosing a model that is relatively simple would make the most sense as we do not want to overfit to our data. Starting off with a Logistic Regression model would be the best choice to start with. Logistic regression is less likely to over-fit low dimensional datasets and makes no assumptions about distributions of classes. However, we may run into issues with the linear boundaries it create and may have to shift to using a different model for our next test. For more information on Logistic Regression, click here. We will also need to Encode our features with One Hot Encoding. For more information on OneHotEncoding, click here.

We can the view the results with a Confusion Matrix and ROC Curve. These, along with the model itself, are provided by sklearn and offer a great way to implement and view machine learning models. The confusion matrix will help us understand the relationship between False Positives, True Negatives, etc. The ROC Curve will let us know how good our model is at predicting, with values of around 0.5 indicating it is no better than random choice, and values or 1 indicating perfect predictions.

Conclusion¶

From our 4 types of models, we found that it is difficult to predict the winner of a map based on the agents selected. While it may be easy to say this is conclusive evidence toward 'the meta' not existing, there are some caveats we need to account for. For example, since there is a perception of what is 'meta', players tend to pick similar compositions. This makes it difficult to test every possible combination of agents and truly determine which is the best. However, whatever the case may be, to answer our original question, 'what is meta, and does it exist', we can say that for this era of Valorant and with this dataset, there does not seem to be a dominant composition or agent required to win a match. The best option would be to mirror the enemy team composition or simply pick the agents with the highest pickrate for their respective roles.
